Care Area

Preventive checkups

A regular health checkup finds problems early — often before any symptoms appear — when they are simplest and cheapest to treat. Prevention really is easier than cure, and a yearly review gives you peace of mind.

Book an Appointment Call the Clinic

Why preventive checkups matter

Many serious conditions — high blood pressure, diabetes, high cholesterol — develop silently for years. A periodic checkup screens for them based on your age, weight, habits and family history, so they are caught while still easy to manage.

A checkup is also the right time to update vaccinations, review any long-term medicines and talk through lifestyle changes that lower your future risk. It turns vague worry into a clear, personal plan.

What a checkup covers

  • History and physical examination
  • Blood pressure measurement
  • Blood sugar / HbA1c
  • Lipid (cholesterol) profile
  • Basic blood and urine tests
  • Vaccination review and lifestyle advice

How to stay ahead

Get checked once a year, or as advised if you already have a condition or a strong family history. Know your key numbers — blood pressure, sugar, cholesterol and weight — and keep your reports together in one file.

Bring a list of your current medicines and any symptoms you have noticed, however small. Acting on borderline results early is far easier than treating a full-blown problem later.

What to do

  • Have a checkup yearly, or as advised
  • Know and track your key numbers
  • Keep your reports and prescriptions together
  • Stay up to date with recommended vaccinations
  • Share your family medical history with the doctor

What to avoid

  • Don't wait for symptoms before getting checked
  • Don't skip recommended follow-up tests
  • Don't ignore borderline or 'slightly high' results
  • Don't self-interpret reports from the internet
  • Don't stop a long-term medicine without review

When to see the doctor

Book a visit sooner than your next routine checkup if you notice new or unusual symptoms, an abnormal result, are planning a pregnancy, or have a family history of heart disease, diabetes or cancer.
